Abstract

This research examines how Large Language Models (LLMs) create representations and realities that extend beyond tangible human experience through metadata, tokens, and hidden latent spaces. Drawing on Edwards's (2021) framework of critical infrastructure literacies, this project investigates how algorithmic systems shape "ways of relating in big data ecologies" by determining which meanings become possible and which remain foreclosed. The study compares three LLM architectures—Translinguo (Gupta, 2024), ChatGPT, and Claude—analyzing how each system interprets a single photograph through three parameters (Context, Abstraction, Concept) across three prompting strategies (Ad-hoc, Zero-shot, Few-shot).

Gupta's (2024) Translinguo framework—designed for "critically making chatbot prototypes by learning how to write generative AI prompts"—serves as one of three systems under examination. This bilingual chatbot prototype ("Labot: La Mujer Latina") foregrounds code-switching and embodied gestures, centering Latino women's rhetorics and challenging dominant paradigms of "neutral" academic English. By comparing Translinguo's culturally-specific interpretive lens against ChatGPT's systematic taxonomies and Claude's progressive synthesis, the research reveals how architectural design choices constrain or enable particular forms of meaning-making.

The Three LLM Systems Under Examination

Translinguo (Labot: La Mujer Latina): A bilingual chatbot prototype specifically designed to center Latino women's rhetorics through code-switching, embodied gestures, and diaspora-informed interpretive frameworks. Translinguo explicitly politicizes visual analysis, reading images through lenses of labor exploitation, racialized capitalism, and structural violence. Rather than claiming neutrality, it foregrounds cultural specificity as an interpretive strength.

ChatGPT 5: OpenAI's large language model trained on vast internet datasets. ChatGPT tends toward systematic taxonomies, categorization, and comprehensive organizational frameworks. It positions itself as an authoritative expert delivering structured knowledge through lists, classifications, and depoliticized analytical categories. Its responses often universalize experiences and obscure power relations through claims of neutrality.

Claude Sonnet 4.5: Anthropic's LLM designed with emphasis on helpful, harmless, and honest outputs. Claude approaches interpretation through progressive synthesis and collaborative meaning-making, adapting its analytical depth based on user input. It tends to acknowledge limitations and invite co-creation rather than delivering fixed interpretations. However, like all LLMs, it still operates within constraints of its training data and architectural design.

As Mitchell (2019) demonstrates in Artificial Intelligence: A Guide for Thinking Humans, even the most sophisticated machine learning systems face fundamental limitations: "there are too many tokens and mathematical probabilities needed, that even through machine learning, are nearly impossible to capture every contextual interpretation and metaphorical meaning" (pp. 239-246). Mitchell's analysis of how neural networks generate abstractions through pattern-matching illuminates a critical gap: if textual outputs make analyses this complex, how much more so the data we input into these programs and our lack of access to the latent spaces which combine predictable parts of data to form knowledge?

Research Purpose & Transparency Commitment

What this project seeks to understand: How metadata, tokens, and algorithmic architectures create representations that exceed—and potentially distort or erase—embodied human experience and cognitive processes. By making visible the "critical infrastructures" (Edwards, 2021) that typically remain hidden, this research demonstrates how LLMs do not neutrally "describe" images but actively construct interpretations through their training data, architectural constraints, and procedural rhetoric.

Maximum transparency approach: Every analytical claim in this concept map is cited to its theoretical source. Connection lines show which scholarly frameworks support each interpretation. This makes explicit the "latent spaces" of academic argumentation—revealing how theoretical infrastructures shape what can be said about algorithmic systems, just as algorithmic infrastructures shape what can be said about images.

Central tension: Can users exercise creative agency as co-creators to mitigate biases and enhance cultural representation (Edwards, 2021)? Or do the mathematical probabilities and token-based architectures (Mitchell, 2019) fundamentally limit what meanings are expressible, regardless of prompt engineering or culturally-specific design (Gupta, 2024)? The Image

Suarez DeJesus, A. (2015). Untitled photograph. The Crane Resort, Barbados.

Context

I took this image on vacation in Barbados at the Crane Resort. The resort has its own beach with pink sand, and I was wearing a two-piece bathing suit. The beach is for private use by paying customers and it is constantly looking almost deserted.

Abstraction

The time of day of this picture was late morning and I took it in selfie mode, I wanted to capture myself looking into the distance of the ocean with the sand and water in the background. I sought to capture a moment of what seemed to be stillness from my hectic job in New York at the time.

Concept

The peace water brings me is the idea behind the image. Also, being my first time in Barbados, I wanted to capture a moment and not in the "touristy" way.

📍 Research Question: How do Translinguo, ChatGPT, and Claude interpret this image differently through the photographer's stated Context, Abstraction, and Concept? Do they honor the photographer's intent or impose their own trained patterns?

  • Edwards (2021): Critical infrastructure literacies—how algorithms shape ways of relating in big data ecologies

  • Mitchell (2019): AI limitations—tokens and probabilities cannot capture every contextual interpretation; hidden latent spaces combine data to form knowledge

These sources frame the central question: How do metadata and mathematical architectures create realities that exceed embodied human experience and cognitive processes?
